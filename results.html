Results of the research

Summary

AIMS

This work aims to discover the relationship between salience and lobby tactic choice for the development of tobacco control policies in the European Union.

METHODS

The research (1) measures variability in salience using mass media coverage and Google Trends metrics to measure political salience, which (2) is later compared to findings about the tobacco industry involvement in the 2014 Tobacco Products Directive development process. The research differentiates between 5 types of lobby tactics: direct inside tactics, 3rd party tactics, outside tactics, legal threats and actions, and financial incentives.

RESULTS

This study discovers that salience has little to no effect on the use of inside lobby tactics and has a detectable inverse relationship with the use of outside lobby techniques. Findings related to the other tactics are inconclusive.

CONCLUSIONS

The findings allow to expect a decrease in the pro-tobacco lobby actors' use of outside tactics during spikes in political salience around the legislative issue; it is reasonable to expect a reverse effect as well when salience is low. As for the inside lobby tactics (excluding third-party techniques), the analysis of the findings discourages from expecting to find a strong relationship between their employment by the tobacco industry and political salience in the future.

Hypotheses

The research develops 5 hypotheses based on the 5 identified types of lobby tactics: direct inside tactics, 3rd party tactics, outside tactics, legal threats and actions, and financial incentives.

H1
An increase in the issue salience makes inside lobby tactics, excluding the ones involving the employment of third parties, less likely to be used.

H2
An increase in the issue salience makes the employment of third parties more likely to be used.

H3
An increase in the issue salience makes outside lobby tactics more likely to be used.

H4
An increase in the issue salience makes legal threats or actions more likely to be pursued.

H5
An increase in the issue salience makes financial incentives less likely to be resorted to.

Findings

H1 H2 H3 H4 H5
